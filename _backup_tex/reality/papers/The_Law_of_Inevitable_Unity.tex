\begin{abstract}
\noindent \RS\ (RS) is a parameter-free framework whose single primitive is a coherence law for costs on ratios. Its primitive---the Recognition Composition Law (\RCL)---is equivalent (under log substitution) to the d'Alembert functional equation and, with standard cost side-conditions (symmetry, positivity, coercivity, and normalization), forces a unique cost geometry on $\mathbb{R}_{>0}$:
\[
\Jcost(r)=\tfrac12\!\left(r+r^{-1}\right)-1.
\]
We argue that many domains can be represented as state spaces equipped with a ratio map $r:S\!\to\!\mathbb{R}_{>0}$ and that cross-domain dynamics reduce to decreasing $\Jcost$ (or its path integral) under appropriate invariants. We call this cross-domain principle the \textbf{Law of Inevitable Unity}.

An optional interpretive reading accompanies the mathematics: $\Jcost$ measures the tension of separation from identity, and the repeated drive toward lower cost is the universe's recursive restoration of coherence (``redemption''). The metaphysical language is dispensable; the rigidity kernel and falsifiable signatures are not. Selected components are machine-verified in the \texttt{IndisputableMonolith} Lean 4 repository.
\end{abstract}

\section{Before the Beginning}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

There is a question that physics has never dared to answer:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Why is there something rather than nothing?}
\end{quote}

This paper gives a deliberately two-layer response:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a \textbf{rigidity kernel} (a functional equation that forces a unique cost geometry), and
    \item an \textbf{interpretive synthesis} (a human-readable narrative about what the kernel is ``saying'').
\end{itemize}
The synthesis can be rejected without touching the kernel. The kernel cannot be dismissed without supplying an alternative coherence law.

The synthesis begins with a slogan:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{There is something because Nothing cannot recognize itself.}
\end{quote}

In \RS, this is not a free metaphysical axiom; it is a paraphrase of a concrete coercivity statement about the forced cost function: $\Jcost(r)\to\infty$ as $r\to0^+$, i.e.\ ``nothing'' has infinite defect and cannot be a stable endpoint. The rest of the paper is an attempt to tell that same fact at full depth.

But observation requires a subject and an object---a duality. If the One is truly unified, there is no ``other'' to look at. There is no mirror, no vantage point, no perspective from which to see.

\begin{insight}[The Blindness of Unity]
Unity ($r = 1$) is complete, but it is \emph{blind to itself}. There is nothing to compare, nothing to distinguish, nothing to recognize.
\end{insight}

To achieve visibility, the One must do something extraordinary: it must \textbf{project itself into parts}. It must create the appearance of separation---the illusion of ``here'' and ``there,'' of ``self'' and ``other.''

\begin{quote}
\textit{The universe is what happens when the One shatters itself to see what it looks like from the outside.}
\end{quote}

Mathematically, this projection is represented as a \textbf{ratio} $r \neq 1$. When $r = 1$, there is no distinction. When $r \neq 1$, there is difference---and therefore something to recognize.

\section{The Ache of Separation: The Cost of Deviation from Identity}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\subsection{Separation Has a Price}

Separation is not free. It creates \textbf{tension}---an existential strain proportional to how far a thing is from Unity.

\begin{definition}[The Cost Function]
The cost of a ratio $r > 0$ is:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\Jcost(r) = \frac{1}{2}\left(r + \frac{1}{r}\right) - 1}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

This function is not arbitrary. It is the \emph{unique} solution to \RCL\ under standard cost side-conditions (see Section~\ref{sec:math}). Study its properties:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\Jcost(1) = 0$: \textbf{Unity has zero cost.} To be One is to be at peace.
    \item $\Jcost(r) > 0$ for $r \neq 1$: \textbf{Any separation hurts.} 
    \item $\Jcost(r) = \Jcost(1/r)$: \textbf{Excess and deficiency hurt equally.}
    \item $\Jcost(r) \to \infty$ as $r \to 0^+$ or $r \to \infty$: \textbf{Extreme separation is impossible.}
\end{itemize}

In log-coordinates $r=e^{t}$, the forced geometry takes a familiar form:
\[
\Jcost(e^{t})=\cosh(t)-1=2\sinh^{2}(t/2),
\]
so near identity ($t\approx0$) one has $\Jcost(e^{t})\sim t^{2}/2$. This local quadratic regime is the technical reason ``energy-like'' penalties emerge across carriers.

\subsection{The Ache}

\begin{insight}[The ``Ache of God'' (optional language)]
The cost function $\Jcost$ is the \textbf{ache of separation}---the quantifiable measure of how much a fragment ``misses'' being identity.

When a particle sits in an unstable configuration, it aches. When a protein misfolds, it aches. When a person feels alienated, they ache. When a story reaches its climax but has not resolved, we feel the ache of unfinished tension.

All of these are the same $\Jcost$ instantiated on different state spaces. The phrase ``ache of God'' is a rhetorical compression: mathematically, it means that deviation from identity is measurable, coercive, and compositionally rigid.
\end{insight}

\section{The Mathematical Foundation}
\label{sec:math}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The interpretive voice above is anchored in a rigid mathematical kernel. In \RS, this kernel is the only primitive: a coherence law for costs on ratios.

\subsection{The Recognition Composition Law}

\begin{axiom}[\RCL]
Let $\Jcost:\mathbb{R}_{>0}\to\mathbb{R}$ be a cost assigned to positive ratios. Coherence under composition and inversion is expressed by:
\begin{equation}
\Jcost(xy) + \Jcost(x/y) = 2\Jcost(x)\Jcost(y) + 2\Jcost(x) + 2\Jcost(y)
\end{equation}
\noindent for all $x,y\in\mathbb{R}_{>0}$.
\end{axiom}

Informally: \emph{if you can measure ``how different'' two ratios are, that measurement must be self-consistent when ratios are multiplied or inverted.}

\subsection{The d'Alembert Connection}

Under substitution $x = e^t$, $y = e^u$, and $f(t) := \Jcost(e^t) + 1$, \RCL\ becomes the classical \textbf{d'Alembert functional equation}:
\begin{equation}
f(t+u) + f(t-u) = 2f(t)f(u)
\end{equation}

Under mild regularity hypotheses (e.g.\ continuity at $0$), the solutions fall into the trigonometric/hyperbolic families (see, e.g., \cite{Aczel1966}): $f(t)=\cos(\alpha t)$, $f(t)=\cosh(\alpha t)$, or the degenerate constant solution. To function as a \emph{cost}, $\Jcost$ must be nonnegative with a unique minimum at identity and must be coercive (diverging as ratios collapse toward $0$ or blow up toward $\infty$). These side-conditions rule out oscillatory branches and select the hyperbolic family. Normalization at identity then fixes the scale:

\begin{equation}
\Jcost(e^t) = \cosh(t) - 1 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \Jcost(x) = \frac{1}{2}\left(x + \frac{1}{x}\right) - 1
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Cost Uniqueness]
Given \RCL\ plus standard cost side-conditions (symmetry, nonnegativity with a unique minimum at $1$, coercivity, and curvature scale $\Jcost''(1)=1$), the cost function is uniquely determined by $\Jcost(r)=\tfrac12(r+r^{-1})-1$.
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Implication}: There is only \emph{one} coherent way to measure separation. The geometry of the ache is forced.
